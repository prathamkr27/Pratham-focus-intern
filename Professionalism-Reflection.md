q1 Have you ever experienced or witnessed great teamwork and professionalism? What made it effective?

 During my time on my university's FSAE team, I experienced a defining moment of great teamwork when we hit a critical roadblock just weeks before a major testing milestone. The data from our car's sensors wasn't matching our initial simulations, which meant our setup was fundamentally off. In a high-stress environment where it is easy to point fingers, our team handled the setback with complete professionalism. We called an emergency sync where we relied on respectful criticism, objectively analyzing the data logs and dismantling the problem without letting egos get in the way. Our sub-team leads clearly communicated exactly what was needed to solve the issue, and our project manager demonstrated essential flexibility by temporarily pushing back a non-critical deadline to give us time to fix the core problem properly. Because we communicated openly and adapted our strict schedule, we successfully recalibrated the setup and passed our testing phase, showing me that effective teamwork is about how a group supports each other and pivots when things go wrong.

q3 How can you help create a positive, fun, and professional work environment?

To help create a positive, fun, and professional work environment, I will turn my goal of appreciating teammates into a concrete action by regularly initiating a "Weekly Wins" or "Kudos" thread in our team chat every Friday, specifically shouting out great code reviews or help I received from QA and the ML engineers. Additionally, to be proactive and keep meetings engaging, I will volunteer to run a quick, two-minute icebreaker or trivia question at the start of our Sprint Retrospectives. Finally, to demonstrate that I take criticism positively, I will proactively share one "lesson learned" or mistake I made during our Agile ceremonies, helping to foster a safe, professional environment where the team feels comfortable learning and iterating together.

Q5 What would it look like to be proactive during your internship?

While being punctual and responsive are essential baselines, being truly proactive during my internship means anticipating the team's needs before being asked. I will demonstrate this by actively monitoring our Kanban board and volunteering to own small, unassigned backlog tasks, such as documenting an existing data pipeline or cleaning up legacy SQL queries, whenever I have downtime. Furthermore, rather than just passively taking notes during meetings, I will proactively reach out to QA and the ML engineers ahead of time to clarify data schemas and edge cases, ensuring I am unblocking teammates and preventing pipeline bottlenecks before they even happen.

Q6 How might you follow up with colleagues or supervisors if you need information/action from them? How would you escalate it further if you don't get a response and when would you escalate?

If I need information or an action from a colleague, my first step will be to reach out asynchronously via email or Discord, clearly stating what I need and my deadline. If I don't receive a response and cannot unblock myself by pulling other tasks from the Kanban board, I will wait approximately 24 hours—or just a few hours if it is a critical blocker for our current sprint—before escalating. To escalate the situation professionally, I will reach out directly to Jeremy via a direct Discord message or request a brief 5-minute audio call, clearly outlining the steps I have already taken, who I am waiting on, and exactly what I need from him to resume my work.
